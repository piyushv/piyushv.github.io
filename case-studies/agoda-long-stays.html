Agoda Long-stays

Web, Android, iOS · 2023

Turning the remote work boom into 2,000+ extra bookings per day.

+2,000 Additional bookings/day
+4% Conversion lift (Phase 1)
+1.6 days Avg. length of stay (Phase 2)

My Role and Team

I led end-to-end UX design across web, Android, and iOS: research synthesis, competitive desk research, sketching, UI design, and delivery across three phased releases.

What I owned:

  • Ran desk research across Airbnb, Booking.com, and other OTAs to find patterns and gaps in long-stay discounting.
  • Synthesized survey findings into user segments, JTBD, and design opportunities.
  • Designed the full touchpoint system: homepage banners, search result nudges, hotel card badges, property page education banners, price breakdown integration, checkout display, and calendar threshold indicators.
  • Defined which stay-length thresholds trigger which interventions.
  • Adapted every design across all three platforms.
  • Proposed the phased rollout (Awareness → Nudge → Education) so we could validate each intervention before investing in the next.

The Opportunity

Post-pandemic, a new kind of traveler showed up. People were living in places, not visiting them. Remote work went mainstream, bleisure travel surged, and Agoda spotted a growth opportunity in long-stay accommodations.

Competitive Desk Research

I looked at how other OTAs handled long-stay bookings: Airbnb, Booking.com, and others that offer extended-stay discounts. The main takeaway: every platform assumed users already knew they wanted a long stay. Airbnb showed weekly/monthly pricing on listings. Booking.com had long-stay filters. But nobody was actively converting short-stay browsers into long-stay bookers within the booking flow.

That was our gap. We could intervene during the existing funnel to surface value users had no idea existed.

The Problem

Agoda's booking flow treated a 1-night stay and a 30-night stay identically. Users couldn't discover that long-stay discounts existed. They couldn't compare pricing across stay lengths. And they had no way to feel confident they were getting the best deal on an extended booking.

The design challenge: Increase long-stay bookings by making discounts discoverable, comparisons effortless, and the value proposition clear across Android, iOS, and web.

Understanding Long-Stay Users

Long-stay users are several different groups with different needs.

  • Remote workers and digital nomads (2–8 weeks): Reliable wifi, workspace, home-like setup.
  • Budget travelers (1–4 weeks): Lowest possible nightly rate.
  • Travelers seeking a change of scenery (1–3 months): New environments, exploration.
  • Extended vacationers (1–2 weeks): Relaxation, amenities, flexibility.

This shaped my design decisions directly. Budget travelers react to discount badges immediately. Extended vacationers need a nudge at the right moment. Remote workers want to compare numbers and feel confident in the value.

Jobs to Be Done

Working with our researcher, I mapped user needs through JTBD:

  1. "Help me find a long-stay deal without searching for hours." Discounted rates needed to be surfaced, not buried in price breakdowns.
  2. "Let me compare: is 7 nights actually cheaper per night than 5?" The math wasn't obvious. Users needed visual clarity.
  3. "Make me confident I'm not overpaying." Trust and pricing transparency matter a lot for high-value bookings.
  4. "Let me extend my stay without starting over." Users already enjoying their stay wanted a frictionless way to add nights.

Design Strategy: Awareness → Nudge → Education

I proposed a phased rollout tied to the user awareness model:

  1. Awareness. Help users discover that long-stay discounts exist.
  2. Nudge. Motivate users near a discount threshold to extend their stay.
  3. Education. Help users understand that discounts grow with stay length. Build confidence. Reduce cancellations.

Why phased? Each phase could be measured on its own. If Phase 1 badges didn't move bookings, we'd rethink before investing in Phase 2 banners. This de-risked engineering effort and gave us data to justify continued investment.

Designing the Solution

I designed a connected system of interventions across the entire booking funnel. The targeting logic was central: I identified three threshold clusters where users were close to unlocking a discount tier:

  • 5, 6, 7 days → weekly discount threshold
  • 12, 13, 14 days → bi-weekly discount threshold
  • 26, 27, 28 days → monthly discount threshold

Users at these thresholds had the highest conversion potential. The behavioral gap (1–2 extra days) is small. The financial benefit is concrete.

Phase 1: Awareness

Hypothesis: Users aren't booking long-stays because they don't know discounts exist.

What I designed: "Weekly discount" badges at three decision points in the funnel: search results (where users scan and filter), property details (where users evaluate), and price breakdown (where users justify the purchase).

Design decisions: The badge sits next to the price, not the property name. On Agoda's dense search results, users scan price first. Placing the discount signal adjacent to the number they're already looking at means zero extra effort to notice it. The visual treatment is restrained: a compact badge using a distinct color to differentiate from other metadata without overwhelming the card. I adapted sizing, spacing, and typography across web, Android, and iOS to feel native on each platform while keeping the information hierarchy consistent.

Result: +4% increase in long-stay bookings.

Phase 2: Nudge

Hypothesis: Users near a discount threshold will extend their stay if you show them exact savings, but the intervention has to feel helpful, not pushy.

What I designed: two distinct banner types, a bottom sheet, and a calendar enhancement.

Homepage banner (no dates selected): When users land without dates, they see a general long-stay awareness banner. This plants the idea before they start searching.

Contextual nudge banner (threshold users): On search results and property pages, users who selected dates near a threshold (6, 13, or 27 days) see a targeted nudge showing specific savings for adding 1–2 more days, with a "Learn more" CTA that opens a bottom sheet with the full discount breakdown. The bottom sheet gives depth on demand without disrupting the scan experience.

Calendar dotted line: When a user selects exactly 7, 14, or 28 days in the date picker, a dotted line extends 1–2 days beyond their selection, a visual suggestion that extending slightly would unlock additional savings. It's a hint, not an interruption. You're making the threshold visible in the exact moment they're deciding.

Result: Average length of stay increased by 1.6 days. Users weren't just adding 1 night to hit a threshold; they rethought their trip duration entirely when they saw the value, with many jumping to the next discount tier.

Phase 3: Education

Hypothesis: If users understand the discount and see it reinforced at every step, they'll feel more confident in their booking and cancel less.

What I designed: an education layer woven into the property page, price breakdown, and checkout.

Property page: An education banner shows the actual discounted price for the selected stay length, moving beyond awareness to telling users exactly what they're getting.

Price breakdown: The discount appears as a named line item ("Weekly discount" or "Monthly discount" with the exact amount). For high-value bookings, users scrutinize the price breakdown carefully. Seeing the discount broken out as a distinct saving reinforces the deal.

Checkout: The discount carries through to the commitment moment, where seeing the savings one more time acts as reassurance. When users see the discount at multiple points throughout the funnel, the perceived cost of canceling goes up; they've locked in a deal, and loss aversion works in their favor.

Result: Decrease in cancellation rate.

How the Phases Connected

The three phases formed a funnel of progressive commitment.

  • Phase 1 badges answered: "Does this hotel offer a long-stay discount?" A yes/no signal during browsing.
  • Phase 2 nudge banners answered: "How much would I save by staying longer?" Specific savings with a path to act.
  • Phase 3 education banners answered: "Am I getting a good deal?" Confidence through repeated reinforcement.

A user could hit all three in a single session: browsing search results, they see the badge; they notice they're 1 day short of a discount; they tap "Learn more" for the full breakdown; they adjust their dates; then they see the discount reflected in the price breakdown and checkout. Each touchpoint reinforced the last.

Results & Impact

Through the use of badges, banners, progress bars, and education touchpoints, Agoda was able to increase user awareness and encourage longer stays, resulting in over 2,000 additional bookings per day across mobile apps and web.

What I Learned

  • Surfacing existing value was the highest leverage move. The discounts already existed in Agoda's system. Users couldn't find them. The entire project was about visibility and timing.
  • The threshold targeting expanded the addressable audience significantly. Designing for three clusters (5–7, 12–14, 26–28 days) covered far more users than targeting a single threshold. The 1.6-day average increase suggests many users jumped past the nearest threshold to the next one.
  • The calendar dotted line punched above its weight. The subtlest intervention had outsized impact because it appeared at the exact moment of date selection, the highest-intent interaction in the booking flow.
  • Phased rollout built internal confidence. Phase 1's +4% lift made it easy to get buy-in for Phase 2. Each phase's data justified the next, which mattered when competing for engineering resources.

What I'd explore next: Expand threshold logic to more stay-length ranges. ML-driven personalization for discount recommendations. A/B test nudge timing across different funnel stages. A dedicated long-stay onboarding flow for first-time extended-stay bookers.
