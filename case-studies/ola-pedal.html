Designing Ola Pedal

Product Design · 2018

Ola, the ride-sharing company from India, launched its dockless cycle sharing service called Ola Pedal. Here I take you through the journey of designing Pedal.

Problem Statement

People have to spend a lot of time walking inside campuses and around offices as transport may or may not be available. As shared cycle services are on the rise, we wanted to test if our users are willing to use them and pay for them.

Research

I started with secondary research and competitor analyses of various cycle-sharing companies to understand the models they follow and the features they provide. After this, I did Gorilla Testing by questioning people at nearby college campuses and in our business park. Key findings:

  • The majority of people travel short distances by walking, some use a car or a shuttle service.
  • People are motivated to walk as they see it as a form of exercise — it saves time by avoiding parking and waiting for transport.
  • Pain points of users who tried cycle sharing: returning cycles to a dock, depositing a security fee, and making a pass.

Scope

After considering all initial user feedback, we decided to try a dockless cycle sharing service. Riders can pick and drop cycles anywhere on the street rather than at a fixed station, offering extreme flexibility and saving the expenses of renting a parking space. We divided the Pedal launch into two phases: Pilot and MVP.

Phase 1 — Pilot

We tested our dockless cycle sharing system with 500 cycles at a college campus in India. We used cheap and readily available manual locks instead of automatic GPS locks (which would take ~2 months to ship), enabling a quick pilot launch.

Challenge: No map since cycles had no GPS, so we designed a new flow without one. Created as a Web View for Android and iOS for faster launch. Turnaround time for design was five days, including user research.

I created multiple sketches to define the basic flow and layout, then iterated keeping it easy to code and in line with Ola style guidelines.

For the in-ride screen, the unlock pin is displayed prominently for the first 15 minutes (which are free). After 15 minutes, a timer shows ride duration alongside the fare for transparency.

1,500 Rides per day from just 500 cycles

This proved our hypothesis that people take cycle rides and are willing to pay for them.

Phase 2 — MVP

We got 3,000 more cycles with app-based GPS locks on campuses and offices. Pedal was integrated into the Android and iOS Ola app. User testing from Phase 1 suggested users had trouble understanding the steps, so we added timely pop-ups and illustrations to help during the booking experience.

Cycles are now shown on the map to display nearby availability. A cycle illustration was added on the booking screen to make it easier for the user to find the cycle number.

~17,000 Rides per month with just 3,000 cycles after launch
