Designing VeVe's Comics Curator

How I built a self-serve publishing platform that scaled VeVe from 3 publishers to 17

Role Lead Product Designer
Sole designer, end-to-end ownership
Duration ~3 months
Platform Web · Desktop-first
Team 1 PM · 1 User Researcher · 1 Engineering Lead · 5 Engineers

Impact

−91% Time to publish (5–7 business days → ~45 min self-serve)
−96% Ops hours per comic (~4.5 hrs → ~12 min QA only)
+443% Comics per month (~35 → ~190)
+467% Active publishers (3 major → 17)
+72 NPS Publisher satisfaction (new baseline)

Context

VeVe is the world's largest mobile-first digital comics and collectibles platform with over 10 million comic transactions. Partnerships with Marvel, Disney, DC, and more.

Every single comic was processed manually by VeVe's ops team through an internal fragmented tool.

Comic details, covers, and page uploads were on separate pages. Creating one comic meant jumping across 4–5 disconnected screens and manually linking records between them.

Publishers had zero access. Everything went through ops via email and multiple touchpoints.

This worked with 3 major publishers shipping about 35 comics a month. The business had signed new partners, the ops team was already at capacity, and the mandate was clear: scale to 10+ publishers without scaling headcount.

My job was to redesign everything, unifying the fragmented tool into something publishers could use directly. It was named Comics Curator.

Discovery

Ops Shadowing

I met with the ops team for a few hours watching them process comics.

Every comic touched many touchpoints across email, Slack, Sheets, and the tool's scattered screens.

That became my core principle: all the information related to a comic should live in the Curator tool.

I documented the full workflow that became my reference throughout.

Publisher Interviews (5 Interviews, 3 Publishers)

The researcher helped with recruiting and scripting. I led interviews and synthesized findings.

"We have every piece of metadata in our spreadsheets. If you let us upload that, we'd save hours every week."
— Led to Excel import.

"I need my editors to upload drafts, but I don't want them pushing anything live."
— Defined the approval workflow.

"We manage hundreds of characters and series. If I can't search and link them to comics, I'm just building another spreadsheet."
— Led to the Categories module.

Design Principles

  • One comic, one place. Details, files, covers, metadata unified in a single view. From ops shadowing: fragmented tables forced manual cross-referencing.
  • Import, don't retype. If it lives in a spreadsheet, upload it. From publisher interviews.
  • Connected catalog. Series, writers, artists, characters as linked entities. From publishers managing hundreds of creators across thousands of comics.
  • Controlled autonomy. Draft, review, approval with clear roles. From publisher needs and VeVe's internal review requirements.

Design Decisions

Unifying the Flow

The highest-impact decision was structural.

The old tool stored details, uploads, covers, and authoring in four separate screens. Ops cross-referenced records manually.

I brought everything inside a single comic. Open a comic in the Curator and you see its details, pages, covers, metadata, status, and edit history in one view. One object containing everything related to it.

Information Architecture

Evolved through publisher interview feedback.

Comics (Unpublished, Published, Authoring) was most-used, so it sits at the top with subsections always visible. Publishers switch between these constantly.

Categories (Series, Writers, Artists, Characters) got its own top-level section. Publishers spend dedicated sessions managing them independent of individual comics, cleaning up names, organizing series, adding characters.

The Add/Edit Comic Flow: Three Steps

Replaced the multi-table experience with progressive disclosure.

Step 1: Details. All metadata in one form — title, issue number, series (linked from Categories), creators (linked from Writers/Artists), description, pricing.

VeVe has two comic types. Digital Comics work like ebooks with unlimited supply. NFT Comics are limited quantity with rarity tiers: Common, Uncommon, Rare, Ultra Rare and Secret Rare, each with its own cover variant.

The form branches on type selection. Pick NFT Comic and the rarity section expands. Pick Digital Comic and it stays hidden.

Inline validation catches errors in real time: duplicate issue warnings, creator name suggestions from the Categories database, pricing inversion alerts, required field flags.

Step 1: Details, all metadata in one form, branching pricing for Digital vs NFT Comic types

Step 2: Import Pages. Upload as PDF (auto-split) or individual images, plus cover variants. Files that lived in a separate table in the old tool are now part of the comic itself.

Step 2: Import, pages and cover variants unified inside the comic, no separate table

Step 3: Review. Summary before submission. Edit any step, then submit to move to Pending Approval.

Step 3: Review, full summary before submission, replacing the old multi-screen cross-referencing

The Unpublished Page

This is where publishers spend most of their time to edit and review comics. A few more table columns were added like cover thumbnail, total issues, Digital Comic price, NFT Comic price, series and the columns can be customised.

Unpublished page: status badges, dual pricing columns, and feedback action for in-tool review communication

Approval workflow for VeVe's internal review: Draft (still working), Pending Approval (submitted for VeVe review), Approved (cleared for publishing).

Feedback button appears on comics with internal review notes. A structured form replacing email/Slack back-and-forth — change requests tied to the specific comic.

Approval states (Draft → Pending Approval → Approved) and the Feedback form that replaced Slack back-and-forth

The Categories Module

I designed four interconnected modules: Series, Writers, Artists, Characters, each is a standalone management view.

The interaction works both ways. Type a creator name in the Add Comic form and it searches Writers/Artists. If it doesn't exist, create it inline so it's available for every future comic.

Categories module: Series, Writers, and Artists as first-class objects. Add once, link everywhere, one canonical spelling

Usability Testing

One round of unmoderated testing, 5 participants, UserTesting.com. Researcher recruited and set up. I collaborated on the script and analyzed findings.

Feedback blended into table.

Looked identical to Edit/Preview/Details. Fixed: distinct icon plus notification indicator for unread feedback.

Approval too slow for batch review.

Reviewers wanted to approve from the list. Fixed: quick approval action in the table row.

Results

~45 min Time to publish (target <1 day)
~12 min Ops hours per comic (target <60 min)
17 Active publishers (target 10+)
190/mo Comics per month (from 35)

Ops reduction freed the team to sign new publishers, catalog grew and comic management unified.

Pending Approval became the most-used filter. Writers/Artists databases accessed multiple times weekly per publisher. Review communication moved from email to in-tool Feedback.
