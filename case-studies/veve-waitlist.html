Veve Waitlist 2.0: Giving Every Collector a Fair Shot at Limited Drops

Role Lead Product Designer
Sole designer, end-to-end ownership
Duration ~3 months
Team 1 PM · 1 User Researcher · 3 Engineers

Impact

+8 pp International participation
−20% Payment abandonment
+35% Addressable market expansion
45%→63% Selected → paid conversion
~76% Auto-pay enablement at full launch

My Role

The waitlist concept came from PM and leadership. I was the sole designer - responsible for turning that direction into a shipped product across three platforms.

I owned the end-to-end UX: waitlist joining flow, auto-pay integration, draw experience, failure states, notification lifecycle, and edge cases. I ran usability testing (5 users, high-fidelity prototypes, UserTesting) and produced all final UI for iOS, Android, and web.

Team: 1 PM, 1 Researcher, 3 Engineers (one per platform), me.

The Problem

Veve's drops worked the same way every time: thousands of users racing to hit "Buy" at exactly 10:00 AM EST.

It punished international users. 65% of successful purchases came from US timezones, even though only 40% of the user base lived there. Collectors in Bangkok were up at 11 PM. London collectors set 3 AM alarms.

It created panic instead of excitement. 45% of users who reached checkout abandoned - wrong item clicked, insufficient gems, or technical timeouts. A 30-second window left zero room for thoughtful decisions.

It eroded trust. Discord was full of timezone complaints, payment failure frustrations, and suspicion that bots always won.

"I set 5 alarms across 3 time zones and still miss it."
"I have the money, but I can't click fast enough."

The Design Shift

The old model rewarded speed. The new model rewards participation.

Before After
Participation 30-second window, fixed time 24 hours, join anytime
Selection Fastest connection wins Transparent random draw
Payment Manual checkout under panic Pre-authorized auto-pay

Our researcher ran interviews that confirmed the direction: users wanted fairness without FOMO - participation without the penalty of missed sleep.

The Join Flow: 5 Frantic Steps → 3 Calm Taps

The old experience required up to 5 interactions under time pressure. I reduced it to three taps: "Join Waitlist" → toggle auto-pay → "Confirm."

The confirmation screen shows a countdown in the user's local timezone and a clear "Leave Waitlist" option. I made the leave option prominent on purpose, when users know they can exit anytime, they're more comfortable committing.

Left: join screen with auto-pay toggle off. Right: confirmed waitlist entry with auto-pay active

Auto-Pay: The Highest-Impact Decision

This single interaction change moved more numbers than anything else in the project.

My first design put auto-pay on a separate screen after joining. During usability testing, I watched participants hesitate - the "payment setup" framing triggered the same checkout anxiety we were trying to eliminate.

I redesigned it as an inline toggle with one line: "You'll only be charged if selected. Leave anytime before the draw."

Auto-pay enablement went from 15% (separate step) to 23% (inline toggle) in testing, reaching ~76% at full launch. That drove selected-to-paid conversion from 45% to 63%.

For users with insufficient gems, I added a one-tap top-up with Apple Pay/Google Pay inline, cutting the old 7-step process to 2.

The Draw & Results Experience

For winners: Celebration state with clear next steps. Auto-pay users see instant confirmation.

Non-auto-pay users get a 15-minute grace period, a push notification, full-screen purchase screen with countdown and one-tap payment. If they don't pay, the item cascades to the next random user automatically.

For non-winners (the majority): I used a red result indicator deliberately. An earlier gray version felt evasive in testing. Red communicates the outcome honestly, and users preferred clarity over cushioning.

Selected state auto-pay processing (left) vs. manual Buy Now for users without auto-pay (right)

Notification Lifecycle

The system is async, so notifications became the primary interface.

Stage Channel Purpose
Waitlist opens Push + Email Inform, no urgency
Draw reminder Push Anticipation, confirm auto-pay
Result Push + In-app Win: celebrate. Loss: honest + forward action
Grace period Push + SMS Urgency for unpaid winners

Batching: Power users joining 5 waitlists get one digest, not 5 pings. Individual notifications only for results.

Quiet hours: Nothing between 10 PM to 8 AM local time except win confirmations. A product built to stop sleep disruption shouldn't send 3 AM notifications.

Edge Cases

Auto-pay failure & grace period

When auto-pay fails (expired card, insufficient gems), the item enters a 15-minute reserved state. A full-screen purchase screen with countdown appears. Users can top up gems inline and complete the purchase in one tap. If unpaid, the item cascades automatically to the next random user.

Auto-pay failure states, technical error (left) and low gem balance (right), each with a clear recovery path

Bot prevention

One entry per KYC-verified account. Bots can't game a transparent random system.

Community Response

After launch, users on X posted about finally sleeping through the night. Discord tone shifted from frustration to relief. People were participating more and enjoying it again.

Results

+8 pp International participation
~25% Payment abandonment (was ~45%)
45%→63% Selected → paid conversion
~76% Auto-pay enablement
+35% Addressable market expansion

The inline auto-pay toggle was the single decision that moved the most numbers. A small change to separate screen to inline toggle drove 76% enablement, which drove conversion, which expanded the market. The biggest impact came from the smallest UI change.

What I'd Do Differently

Social features from day one

Users coordinated on Discord post-launch. A "Join with Friends" feature was a missed viral loop.

Richer loss experience

The market link was a start. Personalized recommendations and price alerts would turn the loss state into a discovery surface.
